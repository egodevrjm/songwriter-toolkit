# Songwriting Playbook — Cross-Genre (LLM Ready)

This playbook gives practical writing moves that can be adapted quickly across Pop, Country, Hip-Hop, Rock, Jazz, and EDM.

## 1) Start With The Song's Job

Before writing any line, define the **job**:
- **Emotional job**: What should the listener feel in one sentence?
- **Social job**: Is this for radio, TikTok, live crowd, sync/licensing, or album narrative?
- **Identity job**: What part of the artist's identity is being reinforced?

A strong brief example:
- "A late-night regret anthem that feels intimate in verse and euphoric in chorus, aimed at 18-30 playlist listeners."

## 2) Hook Design Framework (H.E.A.R.T.)

Build hooks using five levers:
1. **H**igh-contrast idea (before/after, love/fear, stay/leave)
2. **E**asy phrase (5–9 words, conversational)
3. **A**ural signature (rhythm or melodic cell that repeats)
4. **R**hyme/echo (internal rhyme, vowel echo, alliteration)
5. **T**itle lock (hook line should usually be the title)

Hook stress-test:
- Can someone remember it after one listen?
- Can it be shouted by a crowd?
- Does the groove still work if lyrics are removed?

## 3) Verse and Chorus Function

- **Verse** = detail, scene, stakes, progression.
- **Pre-chorus** = tension ramp, harmonic lift, shorter lines.
- **Chorus** = emotional thesis, repeated core phrase.
- **Bridge** = fresh angle (new lyric image, new chord, or rhythm break).

A common failure is putting too much explanation in the chorus. Keep chorus language broad and emotionally direct.

## 4) Melody Mechanics That Convert

### Melodic contour rules
- Verses often sit in a tighter range.
- Choruses usually jump to a higher register or longer sustained notes.
- Repeating a 2–4 note motif gives memorability.

### Rhythm rules
- If lyrics are dense, simplify melodic rhythm.
- Put key words on strong beats.
- Leave silence after high-information lines.

### Motif toolkit
- **Sequence**: repeat motif at different pitch levels.
- **Inversion feel**: flip rise/fall behavior for contrast.
- **Answer phrase**: phrase A asks, phrase B answers.

## 5) Lyric Writing: Specific + Universal

Use this ratio:
- **70% universal emotion** (miss you, afraid, hopeful)
- **30% specific detail** (3AM kitchen light, cracked dashboard, train platform)

### Line quality checklist
- One vivid noun per line when possible.
- Prefer concrete verbs over abstract adjectives.
- Remove filler openers ("just", "really", "that").
- End lines with strong words (nouns/verbs) not weak connectors.

### Rhyme strategy
- Use perfect rhymes sparingly in modern styles.
- Lean on slant rhymes and vowel family rhymes.
- Internal rhyme adds momentum without sounding nursery-rhyme.

## 6) Harmony by Genre (Quick Patterns)

### Pop
- Common loops: I–V–vi–IV, vi–IV–I–V
- Keep harmonic rhythm stable; vary topline rhythm for freshness.

### Country
- I–IV–V backbone with tasteful vi or ii for emotion.
- Melody should feel singable by a room, not just a vocalist.

### Hip-Hop
- Minimal harmonic loops with texture changes.
- Use sample chops, bass movement, and rhythm muting as arrangement hooks.

### Rock
- Power-chord economy; dynamic section contrast matters.
- Use pre-chorus lift and chorus impact via density changes.

### Jazz
- ii–V–I language and substitutions for color.
- Voice-leading smoothness is key for emotional sophistication.

### EDM
- Harmonic simplicity plus timbral evolution.
- Build/drop contrast is often more important than chord complexity.

## 7) Arrangement Architecture

Use "energy blocks" on a 1–10 scale:
- Intro: 2–3
- Verse 1: 3–4
- Pre: 5–6
- Chorus 1: 7
- Verse 2: 4–5 (new element)
- Pre 2: 6
- Chorus 2: 8
- Bridge/Break: 4–6 (reset)
- Final chorus/drop: 9–10

If every section is "8," nothing feels big.

## 8) Genre-Specific Writing Moves

### Pop
- Prioritize title phrase by minute one.
- Keep phonetics bright: open vowels in chorus help lift.

### Country
- Anchor lyric in place, object, and relationship.
- Let the hook often carry a twist of phrase or double meaning.

### Hip-Hop
- Pocket first, bars second.
- Use multisyllabic rhyme clusters and strategic repetition tags.

### Rock
- Build around riff identity.
- Chorus lyric should feel chantable and physically performable.

### Jazz
- Treat melody like a conversation with harmony.
- Use lyric phrasing that allows behind-the-beat interpretation.

### EDM
- Write toplines that survive heavy sidechain and dense synth layers.
- Keep pre-drop lyric concise and rhythmically clear.

## 9) Rewrite Passes (Professional Workflow)

Do 4 separate passes instead of one giant edit:
1. **Structure pass**: Is section order and length right?
2. **Hook pass**: Is the title/hook repeated enough with variation?
3. **Language pass**: Replace clichés with fresh images.
4. **Performance pass**: Sing/rap everything out loud and trim tongue-twisters.

## 10) Co-Writing Protocol

- Session starts with 10-minute brief alignment.
- Pick one "north star" line early.
- Decide lane ownership: topline, lyric, chords, production.
- Capture every alt hook and pre line in a running board.
- End with a rough bounce and next-session TODO list.

## 11) Prompt Templates for LLM Collaboration

### Idea expansion prompt
"Given this title and emotional goal, generate 12 chorus hook options with varied vowel colors and stress patterns."

### Verse tightening prompt
"Rewrite this verse with more concrete imagery, fewer filler words, and one internal rhyme per two lines."

### Genre transfer prompt
"Translate this pop chorus concept into country and hip-hop versions while preserving core emotional message."

## 12) Song Finishing Checklist

- Title appears naturally and memorably.
- Chorus lands by ~1:00 (commercial contexts).
- Section lengths avoid fatigue.
- Melody and lyric stress align.
- At least one unexpected moment (lyric twist, rhythmic break, harmonic surprise).
- Final 10% polish completed (ad-libs, transitions, lyrical micro-edits).
