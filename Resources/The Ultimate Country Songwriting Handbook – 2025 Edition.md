### 1\. The Only Rule That Matters

Country is three chords and the truth. – Harlan Howard (still true in 2025\)

If the listener doesn’t believe you lived it, they won’t care.  
Everything else is technique.

### 2\. The 2025 Nashville Radio Checklist (Distilled from every current \#1)

- Title \= first or last line of chorus (95 % of Billboard \#1s since 2020\)  
- Chorus melody jumps up (fifth or octave) on the title/payoff word  
- First 10 seconds must slap (cold vocal hook or signature lick)  
- Song length: 2:48–3:12  
- Positive or triumphant payoff – even heartbreak songs end defiant  
- Minimum 2–3 Holy Trinity tropes (make them fresh): truck, dirt road, whiskey, mama, God, small-town Friday night, boots, tailgates, cold beer, girl in cut-offs/sundress, bonfire, “raised right,” cheating (but sorry), fighting (but wins)  
- Conversational language – write like you text your best friend at 2 a.m.  
- TikTok moment built in (8–15 second chant or repeatable hook)

### 3\. Destination Writing – The Single Best Lyric Tool Ever Invented for Country (Andrea Stolpe / Berklee)

This is how Zach Bryan, Tyler Childers, Morgan Wallen, Lainey Wilson, and every great modern writer actually write.

Process:

1. Pick a destination (place, person, moment, or specific time)  
2. Set timer for 6 minutes  
3. Write in paragraph form – NO EDITING  
4. Use all senses: sight, sound, smell, taste, touch, movement  
5. Then underline external details (concrete, visual)  
6. Put parentheses around internal details (thoughts, emotions)

Example (Motel 6 off I40, running from something):

External: flickering neon VACANCY sign bleeding red onto cracked asphalt, diesel fumes mixing with Waffle House grease, Bible in the nightstand drawer with someone else’s desperate highlighter marks, the hum of the ice machine down the hall like a dying heartbeat  
Internal: (I still taste your goodbye on my tongue), (if I close my eyes I’m sixteen again and you’re not gone), (Lord if you’re listening this ain’t how the story was supposed to go)

→ These lines go straight into verses/choruses. This is how you get “your perfume’s still on my passenger-seat pillow.”

Do this exercise every day for 30 days and your lyrics will be in a different league.

### 4\. External vs. Internal Detail – The Secret Sauce

Bad country lyric: “I miss you”  
Good country lyric: “Your perfume’s still on my passenger-seat pillow / And that Chevron hat you stole is still in my dash”

External \= shows  
Internal \= tells (use sparingly, like hot sauce)

Best country lines combine both in one image:  
“Your buckle makes impressions on the inside of her thigh” – Tyler Childers (external \+ internal in one devastating line)

### 5\. Hooks: Make Your Main Hook Crucial (Bonnie Hayes)

“If you can remove the hook and the song still kinda works, you failed.”

Country hook rules 2025:

- Title at start or end of chorus  
- 6–10 syllables max per line  
- Repetitive rhythmic pattern  
- Melodic leap on the money note  
- Says something people already think but never said out loud

Examples of perfect hooks: “Last night we let the liquor talk”  
“I had some help”  
“Take me to the bar, son”  
“You look like you love me”

### 6\. Melody & Prosody – Pat Pattison’s Rules Applied to Country (The Art of Setting Words to Music)

Country melodies must match natural speech.

Rules:

- Stress important syllables on strong beats (1 & 3 in 4/4)  
- Unimportant words (the, of, and, to) go on weak beats or tied over  
- Avoid singing “to-MAH-to” when everyone says “tuh-MAY-to”  
- Primary stress in multi-syllable words must land on higher or longer note

Bad prosody example: stressing the wrong syllable in “whiskey”  
Good: every Morgan Wallen chorus ever

Pat’s credo: PRESERVE THE NATURAL SHAPE OF THE LANGUAGE

### 7\. Arrangement Foundations for Country Songs (Bonnie Hayes / Sarah Brindell / 2025 reality)

2025 country arrangements are bigger than ever but still start simple.

Basic tracks order:

1. Acoustic guitar or loop that you wrote the song to  
2. Vocal (even if scratch)  
3. Kick drum on 1 & 3 (or 1 & a2 & a3 & a4 for modern)  
4. Bass that locks with kick

Modern country must-haves by section:

- Intro: 4–8 seconds max, signature lick  
- Verse: stripped (acoustic \+ vocal or light percussion)  
- Pre-chorus: build (add hats, harmony)  
- Chorus: explode (full band, gang vocals, octave doubles)  
- Bridge: half-time or total strip (vocal \+ one instrument)  
- Final chorus: double chorus \+ 1 (key up or add new layer)

Tempo: 70–100 BPM \= emotional ballads  
120–150 BPM \= uptempos  
80–95 BPM \= sweet spot for most radio hits right now

### 9\. Modern Country Sub-Genres & Their Exact Formulas (2025)

Traditional/Red Dirt (Zach Bryan, Childers, Stapleton)  
→ 4–6 minute songs OK, organic production, Destination Writing heavy, imperfect vocals \= feature

Pop-Country Radio (Wallen, Bailey Zimmerman, Shaboozey, Jelly Roll)  
→ 2:50–3:10, huge drums, 808s OK, chant vocals, title at end of chorus

Texas/Outlaw (Cody Jinks, Turnpike, Koe Wetzel)  
→ Telecaster twang mandatory, raw vocals, anti-Nashville attitude

Americana (Sierra Ferrell, Billy Strings, Isbell)  
→ No rules except authenticity, long songs OK, storytelling ballads

90s Throwback (Lainey Wilson, Megan Moroney)  
→ Fiddle \+ steel mandatory, big singalong choruses, bell-bottom jeans energy

### 10\. Final Checklist Before You Demo

- Can a drunk person sing the chorus after one listen?  
- Does the first 10 seconds make you turn it up?  
- Is there a moment that would kill on TikTok?  
- Did you use Destination Writing somewhere in the lyric?  
- Does the singer sound like they’ve lived every word?  
- Would an 18-year-old girl and a 55-year-old truck driver both turn it up?

If yes to all → send it to publishers.
